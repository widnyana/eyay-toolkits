# Output Formats

Guidance for each output mode. Select based on what the user asks for — or default to vignette when unspecified.

---

## Vignette (Default)

Short, scene-based prose. One moment, rendered precisely. Usually 150–350 words.

**Structure**:
1. Ground the reader in a specific, mundane detail (the room, the Zoom grid, the Slack message)
2. Let the absurdity build through accumulation or contrast
3. End on a beat that lands — dry, ironic, or unexpectedly true

**Tone**: Observational. The narrator is present but not editorialising. Let the scene do the work.

**Example opener patterns**:
- "By the third time Marcus said 'alignment', no one was aligned on what alignment meant."
- "The retrospective had thirty sticky notes in the 'what went well' column and four in the 'what could be better' column. No one mentioned the project had shipped two weeks late."
- "She had been on mute for eleven minutes before anyone noticed."

**What to avoid**:
- Don't explain the joke. The observation is the punchline.
- Don't name the archetype explicitly ("the middle manager did..."). Show it.
- Don't editorialize with words like "absurdly" or "ironically" — let the irony be structural.

---

## Blog / Article

Full satirical essay. 400–900 words. Written from an insider's perspective — not a pundit.

**Structure**:
1. **Lede**: One specific incident or observation that opens the larger point
2. **The Thing Everyone Knows But Doesn't Say**: The gap between official narrative and lived reality
3. **Anatomy of the Absurdity**: 2–3 specific patterns, named and illustrated
4. **The Twist or the Lesson**: Something honest that cuts through — not moral, just true
5. **Closing line**: Should land flat, not inspirational

**Tone**: Dry, familiar. Written as if by someone who survived the thing and found it funny in retrospect.

**What to avoid**:
- No listicle headlines ("7 Ways Your OKRs Are Lying to You")
- No think-piece gravity ("In today's complex landscape of work...")
- No resolution. These essays don't fix things. They observe them.

---

## Dialogue / Script Snippet

Scene-based exchange between characters. 100–300 words. No stage directions needed unless they're doing work.

**Structure**:
- No more than 3 characters at once
- Each character should be distinguishable by register, not by name alone
- Let the subtext do the actual work — what no one says is often the point

**Tone**: Theatrical naturalism. These are real conversations that no one would write down but everyone has heard.

**Example exchange structure**:
```
MANAGER: So I just want to make sure everyone feels heard.
IC: About the reorg?
MANAGER: I wouldn't call it a reorg. It's more of a... recalibration.
IC: Of our reporting lines.
MANAGER: Among other things.
[pause]
IC: So a reorg.
MANAGER: I want to make sure we frame this correctly.
```

**What to avoid**:
- Over-explaining in dialogue ("As you know, Bob, the Q3 OKRs are...")
- Comedy that requires the character to be stupid — they should be smart and still wrong
- Resolution or catharsis — the scene ends mid-absurdity, not after it

## Social Media Post

Platform: LinkedIn by default (unless specified otherwise). 80–250 words.

**Structure**:
1. **Opening hook**: An observation, a "hot take", or a confessional opener — written in LinkedIn's particular dialect of sincerity
2. **The turn**: The moment it becomes something else — ironic, pointed, accidentally revealing
3. **Closing**: Either an earnest call to action (played straight for irony) or a flat statement that undercuts everything before it

**Tone**: Perform the genre, then subvert it. The best LinkedIn satire reads like LinkedIn until it doesn't.

**Example patterns**:
- Genuine insight framed in corporate-speak until the mask slips
- The vulnerable story that is slightly too convenient to be real
- The hot take that is just a description of basic professional competence
- The "unpopular opinion" that is extremely popular

**What to avoid**:
- Explicit parody markers ("This is a joke"). Let the genre do it.
- Too mean — the target is the culture, not the person
- Losing the register entirely — if it no longer reads like LinkedIn, it doesn't work

---

## Selecting a Format

When the user doesn't specify:
- Default to **vignette** — it's the most flexible and usually the most effective
- If the user gives a specific setting + event, vignette or dialogue fits
- If the user wants "a post about X" or "something I could share", use **social media**
- If the user gives a topic to explore more broadly, use **blog/article**
- If the user references characters or wants a scene, use **dialogue**

It's valid to offer a second format unprompted if a different one would land better — just say so.
